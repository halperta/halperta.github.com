11 Apr. 2016
Extracurriculurs

An article out in the Chronicle today tells us that undergraduates are in an extracurricular arms race. They do too many extracurriculars, writes William Hurst, and it's destroying their education.

I was surprised to see the article because just yesterday I gave an impromptu monologue about how much I admire my undergraduate students for precisely this choice.

My speculation: I think very few students go to college primarily to learn interesting things. Instead, they go to college to develop skills that will help them in the workplace. In this way, they are not so different from the young man at Harvard in 1840 whose correspondence I'm reading right now. He, too, saw college as one step on the road to a professional degree, and one that he did not take too seriously. He preferred the bars and dances to Latin and Greek. As a lawyer, however, his classical education would ultimately provide him with cultural capital. This is less certain in the professions that most undergraduate students, even among elite liberal arts colleges, pursue today.

My students expect to come out of college looking like employees. Classrooms are rarely able to help with that, in part because choices we make as instructors, but extracurricular activities can. Employers look for resumes full of valuable experiences, which classwork on its own cannot provide. They seek hard skills like grant writing, programming, or design, many of which are not available in the classroom. They also look for professional experience with teamwork, project management, and budgets. The same, not coincidentally, is true of the employers that most PhD students in the liberal arts will face after graduating. If classrooms, especially in the liberal arts, do not value these things, students will look elsewhere. As they should.

It can be frustrating, as an instructor, to have students fail to complete coursework. One solution is to reorient the classroom towards professional goals. Design assignments that students can talk about in a job interview. Assess teamwork with the same criteria that might be used in a professional workplace. Align your curriculum with the professional world.

This makes some faculty uneasy, and for good reason. Perhaps the task of the liberal arts classroom is not to prepare a student for their entry level job, but rather for the job they may want in five or ten years, when a solid grounding in research, methods, and critical thinking is more highly valued. In that case, the recommendations made by Hurst may be appropriate. But the incredibly hard-working, driven, brilliant students that I know will always be forced to chose between a surplus of opportunities. And a classroom that doesn't share their priorities is not likely to win their full attention.

7 Feb. 2016
A Beginner's Guide to Presenting at MLA

January was both my first time attending MLA, and also my first time presenting at MLA. I came prepared to give the same kind of conference presentation I have given at ACLA, TXDHC, and GRACLS. The talk was oriented around a single argument, divided into two sub-arguments, presented visually and without reading over the course of 18 minutes.

I found that the format didn't fit the forum, and so I thought I would write what I learned for those who may come after me.

1. Be general: Many panels at MLA feature speakers who have been thinking about the ideas, questions, concerns, provocations that they bring to the table for decades. The content, then, tends to be broad, and the tone tends to be inquisitive. Replace the tripartite argument of a seminar paper with a query that involves only very brief, very accessible close-readings. Reduce theory to the simplest of misrepresentations. End on an up-tick that may or may not be a question.

2. Be sociable: Many panels at MLA are organized almost a year in advance, responding to conversations that have been going on for decades. If you don't know the conversation, your presentation may appear peripheral. But you can resolve this with a little research into your panel and their work. Know the panelists, anticipate their arguments, and speak directly to their claims whenever possible.

3. Be short: no more than fifteen minutes. Everyone is tired.

4. Don't wear jeans: I wore (very nice) jeans (with a blazer) on my firt day at MLA and two strangers, both male tenured faculty, commented separately on the choice. It's a battle that's not worth fighting.
